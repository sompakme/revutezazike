Ingrid Bergman (born August 29, 1915, died August 29, 1982) was a Swedish actress who appeared in a number of European and American films during her career. She received three Academy Awards, two Emmy Awards, four Golden Globe Awards, and the Tony Award for Best Actress for her performances. In a poll conducted by the American Film Institute, she was named as the fourth greatest female star in American movie history. Most notably, she is renowned for her appearances as Ilsa Lund in Casablanca (1942), a World War II drama in which she appeared with Humphrey Bogart, and as Alicia Huberman in Notorious (1946), an Alfred Hitchcock thriller in which she appeared alongside Cary Grant.  She had previously worked as a prominent actress in Swedish films before breaking out as a star in American cinema. After appearing as the lead in the English-language version of the Italian film Intermezzo in 1939, she became well-known in the United States. With her “Nordic freshness and vitality” on the screen, as well as her exceptional beauty and intelligence, she quickly established herself as “the ideal of American womanhood” and one of Hollywood’s greatest leading actresses, according to the St. James Encyclopedia of Popular Culture (St. James Encyclopedia of Popular Culture).  Following her performance in Victor Fleming’s adaptation of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde in 1941, she caught the attention of her future producer David O. Selznick, who described her as “the most thoroughly conscientious actor” he had ever had the pleasure of working with. In response to her demand, he began with a one-film part and, after that, signed a four-film deal (also at her insistence) rather of the customary seven-year acting contracts generally negotiated with international performers at the time, therefore ensuring her continuous popularity.  In addition to Casablanca, she appeared in a number of other films, including For Whom the Bell Tolls (1943), Gaslight (1944), The Bells of St. Mary’s (1945), Alfred Hitchcock’s Spellbound (1945), Notorious (1946), and Under Capricorn (1949), as well as the independent movie Joan of Arc (1948). Having achieved success in American films for a decade by 1950, she featured in the Italian film Stromboli. The picture, which she acted in, resulted in a love affair with director Roberto Rossellini, who was married at the time. The controversy surrounding her romance and subsequent marriage to Rossellini caused her to stay in Europe until 1956, when she made a spectacular Hollywood comeback with Anastasia, for which she received her second Academy Award and the forgiveness of her admirers. She has a large collection of personal and film documentation, which may be found at the Wesleyan University Cinema Archives.
